Predicting Travel Delays¶

by Robert Hofstetter¶

Investigation Overview¶

In this notebook I will show one way to predict flight arrival delays to help carriers make recommendations to consumers about how much time to allow for connecting flights when they are in the booking process.

Dataset Overview¶

The data for this analysis was obtained from the Bureau of Transportation Staticis. All domestic United States regions for years 2018 and 2019 were download by month and concatenated together outside of this notebook into two separate files for each year. It is a rather large set of data so I've separated the cleaning in order to speed processing of cells.

Average Delays¶

Note that when an arrival delay does occur there are none less than 15 minutes and the mean seems to be 30 minutes with extremes as high as 1,920 minutes.

Number of Trips¶

It turns out the number of trips made between cities is a factor in predicting the anticipated arrival delay when a delay does occur. When it is a major trip with a single daily flight the mean delay is around 30 minutes whereas the mean for very frequent trips is as high as 60 minutes.

Comparing Delays and Trips across Carriers¶

Many carriers follow the pattern of more trips equates to longer delays but there are outliers. JetBlue can anticpate shorter delays when a segment makes ten trips and Delta can anticipate the longest delays when they operate a segment that makes eight trips.
